Digital Health Advice Accountability Act, 2026
Full Legislative Text — Draft Proposal
Proposed by: Dr Prakash Kumar, Founder, Digital Health Accountability Forum India (DHAF)
Co-Proposed by: Dr Pallavi Kusum, Co-Founder, Digital Health Accountability Forum India (DHAF)
Origin: Patna, Bihar, India
Year of Origination: 2025
Proposed Enactment Year: 2026
Document type: Citizen-originated draft legislation for Parliamentary consideration
Preamble
WHEREAS the Constitution of India guarantees under Article 21 the right to life with dignity, which includes the right to safe, accurate, and accountable health information;
WHEREAS digital platforms including search engines, artificial intelligence systems, chatbots, and health applications now function as the primary source of health information for over 600 million Indian citizens;
WHEREAS these platforms interpret symptoms, suggest diagnoses, recommend treatments, name medicines, influence urgency decisions, and rank healthcare providers — each of which constitutes direct influence over medical decisions at population scale;
WHEREAS no existing Indian legislation — including the National Medical Commission Act 2019, the Indian Medical Council Act 1956, the Consumer Protection Act 2019, the Information Technology Act 2000, or the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 — establishes any licensing, accuracy, liability, or accountability framework for digital platforms providing health advice;
WHEREAS licensed medical professionals in India are subject to rigorous qualification requirements, licensing, audit, peer review, and full civil and criminal liability for medical advice — creating a profound and unjustifiable double standard;
WHEREAS countries including members of the European Union have begun regulating high-risk artificial intelligence in healthcare, and India has both the constitutional mandate and the digital infrastructure to lead this regulatory domain globally;
NOW THEREFORE it is hereby proposed that the Parliament of India enact the following legislation:
Part I — Preliminary
Section 1 — Short Title and Commencement
1.1 This Act shall be called the Digital Health Advice Accountability Act, 2026.
1.2 It shall extend to the whole of India.
1.3 It shall come into force on such date as the Central Government may, by notification in the Official Gazette, appoint. Different dates may be appointed for different provisions.
Section 2 — Definitions
In this Act, unless the context otherwise requires:
2.1 "Digital Health Entity" means any person, company, platform, application, or system — whether incorporated in India or outside India — that provides health-related information, advice, diagnosis suggestions, treatment recommendations, medicine names, urgency guidance, or healthcare provider rankings to users located in India, through any digital medium including but not limited to:
Search engines
Artificial intelligence chatbots
Mobile health applications
Social media platforms
Video platforms
Telemedicine platforms operating without licensed physician oversight
2.2 "Health Advice" means any output — textual, audio, visual, or generated — that:
Interprets a symptom described by a user
Suggests a possible diagnosis
Recommends a treatment, medicine, or procedure
Advises on urgency of seeking medical care
Ranks or recommends healthcare providers based on non-clinical criteria
2.3 "AI Health System" means any artificial intelligence model, large language model, machine learning system, or automated response system that generates health advice as defined above.
2.4 "Digital Health Entity Licence" (DHEL) means the licence issued under Section 5 of this Act authorising a Digital Health Entity to provide health advice to users in India.
2.5 "NMC-AI Medical Accuracy Board" (NAMAB) means the independent regulatory body established under Section 7 of this Act.
2.6 "Red-Flag Symptom" means any symptom included in the schedule maintained by NAMAB indicating potential medical emergency requiring immediate professional evaluation.
2.7 "Health Query Data" means any data — including search terms, prompts, questions, or inputs — submitted by a user to a Digital Health Entity that relates to symptoms, diagnoses, treatments, medicines, healthcare providers, or health conditions.
2.8 "Patient Harm" means any physical, psychological, or financial harm suffered by a person that is causally linked to health advice provided by a Digital Health Entity.
Part II — Licensing
Section 3 — Prohibition Without Licence
3.1 No Digital Health Entity shall provide health advice to users in India without obtaining a valid Digital Health Entity Licence (DHEL) issued under this Act.
3.2 Any Digital Health Entity operating without a DHEL shall be liable to:
Immediate suspension of operations in India
A penalty of ₹50 crore for first violation
A penalty of ₹200 crore for repeat violation
Criminal prosecution of responsible officers under Section 18 of this Act
Section 4 — Application for Digital Health Entity Licence
4.1 Any Digital Health Entity seeking to provide health advice in India shall apply to the NMC-AI Medical Accuracy Board in the prescribed form.
4.2 The application shall include:
Full details of the platform and its health advice mechanisms
Technical documentation of AI models used, if applicable
Results of internal accuracy testing
Data handling and privacy compliance certification
Emergency referral protocol documentation
4.3 The Board shall process applications within 90 days of receipt.
Section 5 — Grant and Conditions of Licence
5.1 The Board may grant a DHEL subject to the following mandatory conditions:
Maintenance of 90% or above accuracy on standardised clinical scenario benchmarks
Full compliance with the Mandatory Safe Referral Protocol under Section 9
Full compliance with Health Query Data Protection under Section 11
Prohibition on paid ranking in health search results under Section 13
Mandatory disclosure of AI-generated content under Section 14
Submission to annual accuracy audit under Section 8
5.2 Licences shall be valid for one year and renewable annually upon satisfactory audit.
5.3 The Board may suspend or revoke a licence at any time upon finding of material non-compliance.
Part III — The NMC-AI Medical Accuracy Board
Section 6 — Establishment of NAMAB
6.1 The Central Government shall establish an independent body called the NMC-AI Medical Accuracy Board (NAMAB) within 180 days of the commencement of this Act.
6.2 NAMAB shall be constituted as follows:
5 licensed medical professionals nominated by the National Medical Commission
2 licensed dental professionals nominated by the Dental Council of India
3 technologists with expertise in artificial intelligence nominated by the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology
2 patient advocates nominated by the Central Government
1 legal expert in healthcare and technology law
1 representative from the Indian Council of Medical Research
6.3 The Chairperson shall be a senior licensed physician appointed by the Central Government.
Section 7 — Functions of NAMAB
7.1 NAMAB shall:
Develop and maintain standardised clinical scenario benchmarks for AI health accuracy testing
Issue, renew, suspend, and revoke Digital Health Entity Licences
Conduct annual accuracy audits of all licensed Digital Health Entities
Maintain and update the Red-Flag Symptom Schedule
Investigate complaints of patient harm linked to digital health advice
Publish annual public reports on the state of digital health accuracy in India
Represent India in international forums on AI health regulation
Section 8 — Annual Accuracy Audit
8.1 Every licensed Digital Health Entity shall submit to an annual accuracy audit conducted by NAMAB or its authorised auditors.
8.2 The audit shall test the entity's health advice outputs against the standardised clinical scenario benchmark.
8.3 Any entity scoring below 90% accuracy shall:
Be placed on immediate probation
Be required to withdraw health advice functions within 30 days
Submit a remediation plan within 60 days
Undergo re-audit within 120 days
Part IV — Patient Safety Protocols
Section 9 — Mandatory Safe Referral Protocol
9.1 For any Red-Flag Symptom as defined in the Schedule maintained by NAMAB, a Digital Health Entity must display the following before any other content:
"This symptom may indicate a medical emergency. Please contact a licensed doctor, call 112, or go to your nearest hospital immediately."
9.2 This warning must be displayed:
In the language of the user's interface
In a font size no smaller than the body text of the response
Before any other health information is displayed
9.3 Failure to display this warning in a case where a user subsequently suffers patient harm shall constitute criminal negligence under Section 304A of the Indian Penal Code, in addition to civil liability under Section 16 of this Act.
Section 10 — Prohibition of Diagnosis Claims
10.1 No Digital Health Entity shall claim or imply that its output constitutes a medical diagnosis.
10.2 All health advice outputs must carry a visible, standardised disclaimer in all 22 scheduled languages of India:
"This is AI-generated health information. It is not a medical diagnosis. Consult a registered medical professional before making any health decision."
Part V — Data Protection
Section 11 — Health Query Data as Sensitive Personal Data
11.1 Health Query Data as defined in Section 2.7 of this Act is hereby classified as sensitive personal data under the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023.
11.2 No Digital Health Entity shall:
Use Health Query Data for advertising targeting
Share Health Query Data with insurance companies or employers
Sell Health Query Data to any third party
Use Health Query Data to train commercial AI models without explicit informed consent
11.3 Violation of this Section shall attract:
First offence: ₹100 crore penalty and mandatory data deletion
Repeat offence: ₹250 crore penalty and licence revocation
Part VI — Medical Ranking and Advertising
Section 12 — Prohibition of Paid Medical Rankings
12.1 No Digital Health Entity shall allow any healthcare provider — hospital, clinic, doctor, or health product — to purchase higher ranking in health-related search results or health advice outputs.
12.2 All health provider rankings displayed by a Digital Health Entity must be based solely on:
Verified clinical qualifications
NABH accreditation status
Published safety and outcome data
Patient-reported outcome measures from verified patients
12.3 Violation shall attract:
₹10 crore per paid placement discovered
Public disclosure of the violation on NAMAB's website
Suspension of licence pending full audit
Section 13 — Misleading Medical Claims
13.1 No Digital Health Entity shall display, promote, or algorithmically amplify any claim relating to healthcare providers or health products that:
Cannot be verified by clinical evidence
Uses terms such as "best," "top," or "number one" without verified clinical basis
Is paid for by the healthcare provider being described
Part VII — Liability
Section 14 — Digital Medical Liability
14.1 Where a Digital Health Entity provides health advice that is demonstrably inaccurate, and where a user suffers Patient Harm as a direct or contributory result of acting on that advice, the Digital Health Entity shall bear joint civil liability alongside any treating medical professional.
14.2 The burden of proof shall lie with the Digital Health Entity to demonstrate that its health advice met the accuracy standards prescribed under Section 8.
14.3 Compensation shall be determined by the Digital Medical Consumer Court established under Section 15.
Section 15 — Digital Medical Consumer Court
15.1 The Central Government shall establish a Digital Medical Consumer Court within each state within 365 days of the commencement of this Act.
15.2 The Court shall:
Have jurisdiction over all claims of Patient Harm linked to digital health advice
Resolve cases within 90 days of filing
Award compensation to affected patients
Refer criminal cases to appropriate courts under Section 16
15.3 Appeals shall lie to the National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission.
Section 16 — Criminal Liability
16.1 Where a Digital Health Entity knowingly provides false health advice, or knowingly fails to display Red-Flag Symptom warnings, resulting in Patient Harm including death, the responsible officers of the entity shall be liable for:
Imprisonment up to 7 years
Fine up to ₹500 crore
Permanent disqualification from operating health-related digital services in India
Part VIII — International Leadership
Section 17 — India's Global Role
17.1 The Central Government shall direct India's representatives at the World Health Assembly, WHO Executive Board, and relevant international forums to:
Advocate for a global framework for AI health accountability
Share the DHAAA model as a template for international adoption
Establish bilateral agreements on digital health platform accountability with other nations
Part IX — Miscellaneous
Section 18 — Power to Make Rules
18.1 The Central Government may, by notification, make rules to carry out the provisions of this Act.
Section 19 — Overriding Effect
19.1 The provisions of this Act shall have effect notwithstanding anything inconsistent therewith contained in any other law for the time being in force.
Section 20 — Review
20.1 The Central Government shall cause a review of the operation of this Act to be undertaken every two years and shall place the review report before both Houses of Parliament.
Schedule — Initial Red-Flag Symptom List
The following symptoms shall trigger the Mandatory Safe Referral Protocol under Section 9:
Chest pain or tightness
Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
Sudden severe headache
Loss of consciousness or fainting
Sudden confusion or disorientation
Sudden weakness or numbness in face, arm, or leg
Difficulty speaking or understanding speech
Sudden vision loss
Severe abdominal pain
High fever with stiff neck
Seizures
Coughing or vomiting blood
Severe allergic reaction — swelling of face, lips, throat
Suicidal thoughts or self-harm ideation
Jaw swelling with difficulty swallowing or breathing
Severe dental infection with facial swelling
Uncontrolled bleeding
Signs of stroke — face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty
Symptoms of heart attack in women — fatigue, nausea, jaw pain
Any symptom in an infant under 3 months with fever
This schedule shall be maintained and updated by NAMAB on a six-monthly basis.
Statement of Objects and Reasons
This Act is proposed to address a profound and growing public health risk: the unregulated influence of digital platforms over medical decisions made by hundreds of millions of Indian citizens.
The current legal framework in India was designed for a world where medical advice was given by licensed professionals in physical settings. That world no longer exists for the majority of India's population.
Digital platforms now occupy the role of first medical contact for most Indians. They do so without any of the safeguards, qualifications, audits, or accountability that apply to every licensed medical professional.
This Act does not seek to restrict technology or innovation. It seeks to ensure that when technology influences medical decisions — it does so safely, accurately, and with accountability to the patients it serves.
India has the constitutional mandate, the digital infrastructure, and the moral obligation to be the first nation in the world to close this gap comprehensively.
This proposal is offered in that spirit.
