Four Questions That Could Save Your Life
A Patient Safety Guide for Every Indian
Published by: Digital Health Accountability Forum India (DHAF)
Authors: Dr Prakash Kumar (Founder) & Dr Pallavi Kusum (Co-Founder)
Location: Patna, Bihar, India
Year: 2025
"In India, no hospital is required to publish its safety record. No clinic is required to disclose its complication rate. No government portal tracks treatment outcomes. You walk in trusting — without a single number to guide you. That is not healthcare. That is gambling."
— Dr Prakash Kumar, Founder DHAF India
The Reality
Hospitals and clinics in India are not legally required to publish standardised safety information.
The government does not maintain a public database of:
Doctor qualifications by specialty
Diagnosis accuracy rates
Treatment success rates
Complication and infection rates
You are choosing your doctor, clinic, and hospital completely blind.
This guide gives you four questions that cut through that darkness.
Ask them every time. Without apology. Your life is worth that much.
Question 1 — Specialisation
Ask: "What is the exact qualification of the doctor who will treat me?"
Why this matters:
In India, anyone can call themselves a "specialist." There is no public-facing, easily searchable system that lets a patient verify a doctor's actual qualifications before walking through the door.
The difference in training is enormous:

A general dentist performing oral surgery is not the same as an MDS-qualified oral and maxillofacial surgeon.
A general physician managing a cardiac case is not the same as a DM Cardiology specialist.
What to ask specifically:
"What is your degree and from which university?"
"Are you registered with the NMC or DCI? What is your registration number?"
"Have you performed this specific procedure before? How many times?"
What to do with the answer:
Verify NMC registration at: nmc.org.in
Verify DCI registration at: dciindia.org.in
If a doctor refuses to share their registration number — that is your answer.
Question 2 — Diagnosis Accuracy
Ask: "How did you arrive at this diagnosis? What investigations were done?"
Why this matters:
A diagnosis without investigation is a guess. In India, treatment is frequently initiated based on clinical examination alone — without X-rays, blood work, imaging, or specialist referral — because investigations cost time and money that clinics sometimes choose not to spend.
You pay the price when the diagnosis is wrong.
Red flags to watch for:
Diagnosis given within minutes of first examination with no investigations
No X-ray before dental procedures on teeth that cannot be fully examined visually
No blood work before surgical procedures
No referral offered when symptoms fall outside the doctor's specialty
Dismissal of your request for a second opinion
What to ask specifically:
"What investigations did you use to reach this diagnosis?"
"Should an X-ray / MRI / blood test be done before we proceed?"
"Is this within your area of specialisation, or should I see a specialist?"
"What else could this symptom indicate?"
Your right:
You have the right to a second opinion. Always. No doctor can ethically discourage this.
Question 3 — Treatment Success Rate
Ask: "What is your success rate for this procedure? Can you share patient outcomes?"
Why this matters:
In India, no hospital or clinic is legally required to publish procedure-specific success rates. There is no national outcomes database. There is no government portal where a patient can compare the track record of two surgeons.
You are choosing blind.
In contrast:
The UK's NHS publishes surgeon-level outcome data publicly
The USA's Leapfrog Group publishes hospital safety grades
India publishes nothing
What to ask specifically:
"How many times have you performed this procedure?"
"What is your success rate? What does 'success' mean in this context?"
"What does recovery look like for most of your patients?"
"Can you connect me with a previous patient who had this procedure?"
What a good answer looks like:
A confident, experienced clinician will answer these questions directly. They will have numbers. They will have examples. They will not be offended.
What a bad answer looks like:
Vague reassurances. "Don't worry." "We do this all the time." No specific data. Irritation at being asked.
Question 4 — Complication Rate
Ask: "What is your complication rate for this procedure? What is your infection control protocol?"
Why this matters:
Hospital-acquired infections in India are severely underreported. Post-procedure complications are frequently concealed rather than disclosed. Patients are rarely told about complication risks in plain language before procedures.
The sterilisation standards vary enormously between clinics — particularly in Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities where regulatory oversight is weakest.
What to look for in a clinic:
Autoclave sterilisation for all instruments — ask to see it
Single-use items for procedures that require them — needles, syringes, gloves
Staff wearing appropriate PPE
Clean, well-maintained environment
Clear written consent forms explaining risks
What to ask specifically:
"What is your complication rate for this procedure?"
"What sterilisation method do you use for instruments?"
"What happens if I experience complications after this procedure? What is your follow-up protocol?"
"Are you NABH accredited?" (Check: nabh.co)
A note on NABH:
NABH (National Accreditation Board for Hospitals) is India's closest equivalent to a safety certification. Fewer than 1,300 hospitals in India have it. If a clinic is NABH accredited — that is a meaningful safety signal. If it is not — it does not necessarily mean it is unsafe, but your questions become more important.

What DHAF India Is Demanding
These four questions should not have to be asked by patients.
This information should be publicly available — published by every hospital and clinic, maintained in a government database, and easily searchable by every Indian citizen before choosing a healthcare provider.
The Digital Health Accountability Forum India (DHAF) is demanding Parliament enact the Digital Health Advice Accountability Act (DHAAA) 2026 — which includes mandatory public disclosure of all four safety parameters by every licensed healthcare facility in India.
Until that law exists — these four questions are your protection.
